Imagine you are scrolling through a webpage and you see a green box with white text inside in the bottom left-hand corner. Immediately, you know that this is a clickable button. Because you are interested, you move your mouse to click the button. Just as your cursor leaves the white background and crosses into the green shape, the button grows slightly in scale and the text turns yellow.

Are these kinds of uses of technology good or bad design? Does the excitement that the visual effect might give justify its uselessness? Design is about more than just functionality, of course, but in what ways does good design expand beyond utility? Does this button effect qualify for one of those ways?

Letâ€™s try an almost Socratic method:

The scale-up on hover button effect for elements that already look like buttons is bad design because it does more than it needs to. Itâ€™s unecessary.

Since when has unecessary been a sufficient qualifier of bad design? Are ornaments on corinthian columns necessary? They're certainly not structural. Aren't they at least beautiful, interesting, or impressive? Donâ€™t each of those categories qualify as necessary to good design? In this printed title page from 1928, we see lots of ornament.1 It may have cultural connotation and impact thus. It may provide pleasure appropriate to the publication. It may draw attention where attention sometimes (even if rarely) is not drawn. It does not communicate the information that the text communicates, but it may help or it may do something else.

Maybe it's better to avoid the word unecessary. A small upscaling of a button on hover is bad design because it doesn't fulfill the intended purpose. In fact, most of the time, it may be used without any intended purpose.
